Sourcing Decisions in Supply Chains

Role · Supplier Assessment · Design Collaboration · Sourcing Planning · Market Sourcing

Q1What is the Role of Sourcing in a Supply Chain? Why is it important?5 marks▼
Sourcing refers to the set of business processes required to purchase goods and services. It is a critical SC driver as it directly impacts cost, quality, responsiveness, and risk across the entire supply chain.

Key Roles of Sourcing

  • Economies of Scale for Suppliers: A firm may not achieve economical production volumes, but suppliers working across multiple clients can achieve scale in transportation, warehousing, information, and purchasing. (Your notes, Image 1)
  • Cost Reduction: Purchasing activities can represent 50–80% of cost of goods sold in many industries — effective sourcing directly reduces this.
  • Revenue Impact: Good sourcing ensures product availability and quality → directly affects customer satisfaction and revenue.
  • Risk Management: Multi-source vs. single-source decisions determine supply disruption risk.
  • SC Performance: Sourcing decisions affect all SC drivers — inventory, transportation, facilities, and information.

When Does Outsourcing NOT Work? (Your Notes)

  • High Volume: When production volume is large, it is unlikely a 3rd party can get better economies of scale than the firm itself.
  • Highly Variable Requirements: If requirements are highly variable over time, a 3rd party can meet supply through aggregation.
  • Specific Resources: If the service requires specific resources, the firm can hire those resources instead of using a vendor.

Q2Explain Supplier Assessment and Selection. What factors are considered?5 marks▼
Supplier assessment is the process of evaluating potential or existing suppliers to ensure they meet the firm's requirements for cost, quality, delivery, and flexibility — it is the foundation of effective sourcing strategy.

Key Criteria for Supplier Assessment (Chopra Ch.13)

  • Replenishment Lead Time: How quickly can the supplier deliver after an order is placed?
  • On-Time Delivery Performance: What % of deliveries arrive on time?
  • Supply Flexibility: Can the supplier handle demand variability?
  • Supply Quality: Defect rates, quality certifications (ISO).
  • Inbound Transportation Cost: Landed cost of goods at buyer's facility.
  • Pricing Terms: Volume discounts, payment terms, price stability.
  • Information Coordination Capability: Can supplier share real-time data (EDI, VMI)?
  • Design Collaboration Capability: Ability to co-develop products.
  • Exchange Rates, Taxes, Duties: Especially for global sourcing.
  • Supplier Viability: Financial health — will the supplier stay long-term?

Q3What is Design Collaboration in Sourcing? Explain its benefits and challenges.5 marks▼
Design collaboration is when buyers and suppliers work together during the product design phase to improve design, reduce manufacturing costs, and shorten time-to-market by leveraging supplier manufacturing expertise early.

Benefits

  • Suppliers suggest DFM (Design for Manufacturability) improvements — reduces production cost.
  • Reduces time to market significantly.
  • Better component standardization → lower part count, better volume pricing.
  • Reduces engineering change orders (ECOs) after production begins.
  • Better alignment between design specs and actual production capability.

Challenges

  • IP Risk: Sharing proprietary designs can lead to intellectual property leakage.
  • Coordination Costs: Requires tight communication, trust, and dedicated resources.
  • Lock-in Risk: Over-dependence on a single supplier's design capability.
  • Trust Issues: Difficult to collaborate effectively with a new or unproven supplier.

When is Design Collaboration Critical?

  • Complex, technology-intensive products (electronics, automotive components).
  • When supplier has specialized technical capability the buyer lacks.
  • Short product lifecycles demanding rapid innovation.
Link design collaboration to "what makes a good supplier" — IP risk is a major concern worth emphasizing.
Q4Explain Sourcing Contracts — Revenue Sharing, Buyback, and Quantity Flexibility.5 marks▼
Sourcing contracts are agreements between buyers and suppliers that allocate risk and incentivize coordination. Different contract types create different behaviors in the supply chain. From your notes (Image 2), these are key contracts covered in class.

1. Revenue Sharing Contract

  • Supplier charges a lower wholesale price but receives a share of the retailer's revenue.
  • Retailer orders more because per-unit cost is lower → reduces stockouts.
  • Aligns incentives: both benefit when sales are high.
  • Example: Video rental industry — Blockbuster & studios shared revenue.

2. Buyback (Returns) Contract

  • Supplier agrees to buy back unsold inventory from retailer at a negotiated price.
  • Retailer takes more risk off the table → orders larger quantities → reduces stockouts.
  • Supplier bears the downside risk of over-ordering.
  • Downside: No returns allowed means each unit sold is more valuable → OIP.

3. Quantity Flexibility Contract

  • Retailer is allowed to change order quantity up to a specified percentage after seeing actual demand.
  • Reduces retailer risk of committing too early.
  • The retailer is allowed to offer (within limits) to alter quantities; this flexibility is allowed in actuality (within specified limits) and is charged on a variance.

4. Off-Invoice Promotions (from notes)

  • A specified amount of revenue is applied at price — no returns allowed.
  • Each unit sold at a retailer allows the retailer to track revenue — but units are allowed at a specified level of revenue/promotion.

Network Design Decisions

Factors · Distribution Networks · Framework · Facility Location · Uncertainty

Q1What are the factors influencing Network Design Decisions (NDD)? Explain each factor.5 marks▼
Network design decisions determine where facilities are located, their capacity, and which markets they serve. The final decision is taken at a combined consideration of ALL factors. (From your notes, Image 3)

7 Factors (Mnemonic: S-T-M-P-I-C-L)

  1. Strategic Factor: India preferred for many manufacturing plant locations — very stable in market POV. Turkey can connect transportation to Europe as well as Asia. Country-level strategic value matters.
  2. Technological Factor: High-tech preferred. Affected by scale (high or low). High economies of scale → fewer, centralized facilities. Low economies of scale → many, distributed facilities.
  3. Macroeconomic Factor: Must have stable economic market, economic conditions, economic parity. E.g., India's Nov 2016 demonetization (₹500 & ₹1000) made the market volatile → companies reconsidered investment.
  4. Political Situation: Stable & good governance required. Moving from favorable political conditions to volatile situations (due to some reasons in home countries) makes markets volatile.
  5. Infrastructure Factor: Appropriate infra needed. E.g., good network of rail/road/air connectivity, good power availability, good water availability.
  6. Competitive Factor: Where competitors are located influences network decisions. Firms may cluster near competitors for skilled labor pools or locate away to capture new markets.
  7. Logistics & Facility Cost: Excessive logistics & facility cost must not be there. Must balance transportation cost savings with facility fixed costs.
Each factor must have one real-world example. Memorize S-T-M-P-I-C-L. India, Turkey, and demonetization examples come directly from class — use them.
Q2Explain the Cost-Response Time Frontier and Service vs. Number of Facilities tradeoff.5 marks▼
Two fundamental tradeoffs govern network design: (1) Cost vs. Response Time and (2) Service Level vs. Number of Facilities. These are captured graphically in the Cost-Response Time Frontier and the Service-Facilities curve. (Your notes Image 3)

Cost-Response Time Frontier

As response time decreases (faster delivery), cost increases. The frontier shows that for any given response time, there is a minimum cost option. Inventory positioning drives this frontier:

HIGH COST ● Local Finished Goods ← fastest, most expensive ↑ ● Mix (Local + Regional) | ● Regional Finished Goods | ● Local Work-in-Process | ● Central Finished Goods | ● Central Work-in-Process | ● Central Raw Material LOW ● Custom Product at Supplier ← slowest, cheapest ───────────────────────────────→ LOW Response Time HIGH

Key insight: If the customer can wait → no need to locate facility near customer. If response time must be low → must hold local finished goods (expensive).

Service Level vs. Number of Facilities

Response Time ↑ | \ | \ | \_____ (diminishing returns) |____________→ # Facilities

As the number of facilities increases, response time decreases (better service), but at a diminishing rate. Beyond an optimal number, the gain in service is marginal but costs keep rising.

Cost vs. Number of Facilities

  • Inventory cost: Increases with more facilities (safety stock increases).
  • Facility cost: Increases with more facilities (fixed costs).
  • Transportation cost: Decreases initially with more facilities (closer to customers), but inbound transportation may increase.
  • Total cost: U-shaped curve — there is an optimal number of facilities.

Q3Explain the Framework for Global Site Location Decisions (4 Phases).5 marks▼
The framework for global site location provides a structured, phased approach to deciding where to locate facilities globally. It considers competitive strategy, macroeconomic factors, infrastructure, and logistics costs. (Your notes Image 4)

  4. Phase 4 — Location Choices:

    • Influenced by: Logistics Cost.
    • Select the final specific location based on combined total cost analysis.

Q4Explain the Gravity (Centroid) Model for Facility Location with a numerical example.5 marks▼
The Gravity (Centroid) Model is used to find the optimal location for a single warehouse/facility that minimizes total transportation cost, based on coordinates of delivery locations and the volume of goods shipped. (From your notes Image 5)

Model Setup

Given n delivery locations, each with coordinates (x, y) and annual tonnage D:

Objective: Minimize ∑ dₙ · Dₙ · Tₙ Where: dₙ = distance from warehouse to delivery location n Dₙ = annual tonnage to location n Tₙ = transportation cost per ton-mile to location n

Centroid Formula

x* = ∑(xₙ × Dₙ) / ∑Dₙ y* = ∑(yₙ × Dₙ) / ∑Dₙ (x*, y*) = Optimal warehouse location (centroid)

Numerical Example

Worked Example

Three retail locations with coordinates and annual demand:

Procedure (from notes — Gravity Method)

  1. Initially assume x, y = 0 for warehouse.
  2. For every facility, calculate the logistics cost.
  3. Whichever facility gives the minimum cost → that becomes the finalized location.
  4. Three main aspects: ① Role ② Size ③ Location.

Q5What is Network Optimization Model? How is it used in Facility Location?5 marks▼
A Network Optimization Model is a mathematical model used to determine the optimal configuration of supply chain networks — including where to locate facilities, what capacity to allocate, and how to allocate demand. (Your notes Image 5)

What it is Used For

  • Allocate demand to production facilities — which plant should serve which market?
  • Locating facilities & allocating their capacity — where should warehouses/DCs be placed?

Costs Considered

  • Coordination cost
  • Transportation cost
  • Facility fixed and variable cost
  • Inventory cost

In Excel / Solver (from notes)

  • Some data will be given; have to find minimum transportation cost by using Excel Solver to get the location of the warehouse.
  • Decision variable: x and y coordinates of each candidate facility.

Network Design in an Uncertain Environment

  • Environment is not certain — there are lots of uncertainties in the external environment.
  • These uncertainties affect SC decisions. If we don't take uncertainty into account, it is very difficult to develop a responsive supply chain.
  • Challenges: existing environment → Demonetization, Pandemic → e.g. COVID.

Impact of Uncertainty on SC Network Design

  1. SC design decisions include investments in number & size of plants, # of trucks, # of warehouses.
  2. These decisions can't be changed in the short term.
  3. There will be a good deal of uncertainty in demand, prices, exchange rates, and the competitive market over the life cycle of a SC network.
  4. Building flexibility in SC operations allows the SC to deal with uncertainty in a manner that maximizes profit.

Q6Explain Distribution Network Designs: Direct Shipping, Milk Runs, DC with Storage, Cross-Docking, and Tailored Networks.5 marks▼
There are 6 main methods of deciding the TP (transportation) structure/network for delivering goods from manufacturer to customer. (Your notes Image 9)

6 Distribution Network Options

  1. Direct Shipping (Network to Single Destination):
    • Manufacturer ships directly to each customer with no intermediary.
    • Best for: high-value, low-volume products. E.g., Zara direct delivery.
    • Low coordination cost but high per-unit transportation cost.
  2. Direct Shipping with Milk Runs:
    • Same truck visits multiple locations on a single trip.
    • Milk run = same truck, different products, different destinations.
    • Reduces per-unit transportation cost.
  3. All Shipments via Central DC with In-Store Storage:
    • All goods flow through a central DC before reaching stores.
    • DC holds inventory. High storage cost but good consolidation.
  4. All Shipments via DC (Cross-Docking — No Storage):
    • Cross-dock = only repackaging of product, NO inventory stored.
    • Goods arrive at DC, are sorted and immediately dispatched.
    • Low inventory cost. Requires excellent coordination. E.g., Walmart cross-docking.
  5. Shipping via DC Using Milk Runs:
    • After goods arrive at DC, milk run trucks deliver to multiple nearby stores.
    • Best for high-density areas with many nearby customers.
  6. Tailored Network:
    • Uses a combination of above approaches based on customer segment, product type, and geography.
    • Most complex but most efficient — assigns optimal method for each product-customer combination.

Milk Run vs. Cross-Dock (from notes)

Q7Explain the relationship between number of facilities and Cost, Inventory, Transportation (with diagram).5 marks▼
As the number of facilities in a network changes, different cost components respond differently. Understanding these tradeoffs is essential for optimal network design. (Your notes Image 4)

Cost Components vs. Number of Facilities

Cost ↑ | / ← Inventory cost (↑ with more facilities) | / | / Total Cost (U-shaped) | / \____/ | | Facility Transportation (↓ initially) |__|________________→ # Facilities
  • Inventory Cost: Increases with more facilities — more safety stock needed at each location (square root law).
  • Facility Cost: Increases with more facilities — each has fixed overhead costs.
  • Transportation Cost: Decreases initially as facilities move closer to customers (lower outbound cost), but inbound transportation from suppliers may increase.
  • Total Cost: U-shaped curve — has a minimum at an optimal number of facilities.

Service Level vs. # Facilities

Response time decreases as facilities increase, but at a diminishing rate. More facilities → faster response → but each additional facility adds less improvement.

Key Principle

More facilities near customer → Less distance → More # DCs required → Even inbound also loses economies of scale → Total cost goes up after a point.

Q1What is Transportation in a SC? What are the factors affecting Transportation decisions?5 marks▼
Transportation is an important SC driver that refers to the movement of products and goods from one place of production to a place of consumption. It aims to reduce overall cost while increasing transportation service. It also plays a significant component in total GDP. (Your notes Image 6)

Two Factors Affecting TP Decision

  1. Carrier (Party that moves the product):
    • Vehicle related cost
    • Fixed operating cost
    • Trip related cost
  2. Shipper (Party that requires movement of product between 2 points in the SC):
    • Transportation cost
    • Inventory cost
    • Facility cost

Among the Carrier and Shipper, 2 other parties also have significant impact on TP: ① The owner and operators of TP infrastructure such as roads, ports, airports, etc. ② The body that sets TP policies worldwide (government/regulatory bodies).

Modes of Transportation

  • ① Truck (TL and LTL)
  • ② Rail
  • ③ Air
  • ④ Package Carrier
  • ⑤ Water (International trades)
  • ⑥ Pipeline (Liquid and gases)
  • ⑦ Intermodal
Always mention both Carrier and Shipper perspectives. Then list modes — the exam may ask you to compare any two.

Q6Explain Risk Management in Transportation. What are the 3 main risks and mitigation strategies?5 marks▼
Transportation is exposed to multiple risks that can disrupt supply chains significantly. Understanding and mitigating these risks is critical for resilient SC operations. (Your notes Image 12)

3 Main Risks in Transportation

  1. Risk that shipment is delayed — traffic, weather, customs, port congestion.
  2. Risk of destruction — theft, accidents, natural disasters damaging goods in transit.
  3. Risk of hazardous material — dangerous goods causing safety incidents or regulatory problems.

Risk Mitigation Strategies

  1. Decrease the probability of disruption:
    • Use reliable carriers with strong track records.
    • Pre-qualify routes and schedule shipments to avoid high-risk windows.
  2. Alternate Routing:
    • Have backup routes and carriers pre-identified so switching is fast.
    • E.g., if a port is congested, route via alternate port.
  3. In case of Hazardous Material:
    • Use of modified containers, low-risk TP models.
    • Modification of physical and chemical properties can prove to be effective.
    • Follow regulatory compliance (HAZMAT regulations).

Making TP Decisions in Practice (from notes Image 12)

  1. Align TP strategy with company strategy.
  2. Consider both inhouse and outsource TP.
  3. Design a TP network that can handle e-Commerce.
  4. Use Technology to improve transport performance.
  5. Design flexibility into the TP network.
3 risks + 3 mitigation strategies = clean 5-mark answer. Then add "Making TP Decisions in Practice" as a concluding point.

  4. TCO = Total Cost of Ownership (price + transport + inventory + quality defect + risk costs).
  5. Revenue Sharing: lower wholesale price but supplier gets share of retailer revenue.
  6. Buyback: supplier buys back unsold inventory → retailer orders more.
  7. Quantity Flexibility: retailer can change order quantity after seeing demand.
  8. Design Collaboration: buyer & supplier work together at design phase → reduces time to market & cost.
  9. IP Risk is the biggest challenge in design collaboration.
  10. Offshoring = WHERE (another country); Outsourcing = WHO (external party).
★Network Design — 10 Key One-Liners to RememberMust know▼
  1. 7 NDD factors: S-T-M-P-I-C-L (Strategic, Technological, Macro, Political, Infra, Competitive, Logistics).
  2. Cost-Response Time Frontier: faster response = higher cost (local FG = most expensive, raw material at supplier = cheapest).
  3. Service vs. Facilities: Response time decreases with more facilities but at diminishing rate.
  4. Cost vs. Facilities: Total cost is U-shaped — optimal number of facilities exists.
  5. Gravity Model formula: x* = Σ(xn × Dn) / ΣDn ; y* = Σ(yn × Dn) / ΣDn
  6. 4-Phase Global Location Framework: SC Strategy → Regional Config → Desirable Sites → Location Choices.
  7. Cross-docking: Only repackaging, NO inventory storage at DC.
  8. Milk Runs: Same truck, different products, different destinations.
  9. Demonetization (2016) is the classic example for macroeconomic risk in NDD.
  10. Uncertainty in network: SC design investments can't be changed in short term → build flexibility.
★Transportation — 10 Key One-Liners to RememberMust know▼
  1. Two main TP decision parties: Carrier (moves product) and Shipper (requires movement).
  2. 7 Modes: Truck, Rail, Air, Package Carrier, Water, Pipeline, Intermodal.
  3. TL = Truck Load (homogeneous, single customer); LTL = Less Than Truck Load (small freight, multiple).
  4. Rail advantages: fuel efficient, speed, safety. Disadvantages: inflexible, mechanical failure.
  5. Air: fastest, most expensive, best for time-sensitive and sensitive items.
  6. Water/Ship: cheapest per unit, highest volume, slowest, dominant in global trade (automobiles).
  7. Pipeline: for liquids & gases only; very low energy consumption; eco-friendly; inflexible.
  8. Intermodal = multiple modes, MULTIPLE contracts. Multimodal = multiple modes, SINGLE contract.
  9. Piggyback = Rail + Truck; Birdyback = Air + Truck; Fishyback = Water + Truck.
  10. 3 TP Risks: Delayed shipment, Destruction, Hazardous material. 3 mitigations: Reduce probability, Alternate routing, Modified containers.
